Ground Rules of Chess

The Game Clock (Timer)

In competitive chess, the clock (or "timer") is critical. Each player is allocated a specific amount of time to complete all their moves.

  • Running Out of Time: If a player runs out of time, they lose the game immediately, unless the position is a Stalemate or Checkmate, which ends the game instantly.
  • Pressing the Clock: A player must complete their move and then press the clock to start the opponent's time.

This rule governs piece handling: If a player touches one of their own pieces, they must move that piece.

  • Touch-Capture: If a player touches an opponent's piece, they must capture it if possible.
  • Adjusting Pieces: To straighten a piece without moving it, a player must announce "J'adoube" (French for "I adjust") before touching the piece.

Special Rules: En Passant & Castling

Two unique moves that have special conditions:

  • En Passant: A Pawn that moves two squares from its start can be captured by an adjacent opponent's Pawn on the very next turn, as if it only moved one square.
  • Castling: A move involving the King and one of the Rooks. The King moves two squares toward the Rook, and the Rook moves to the square the King crossed. Not allowed if the King is in Check or moves through an attacked square.

Game End Conditions

The game ends in a win, loss, or draw by one of the following methods:

  • Checkmate (Win): The King is attacked and cannot escape.
  • Stalemate (Draw):The current player has no legal moves, but their King is **not** in Check.
  • Draws: Can also occur by agreement, three-fold repetition of a position, or the fifty-move rule.
